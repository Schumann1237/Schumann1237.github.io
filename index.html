Modeling Malaysia's Food Self-Sufficiency

A System Dynamics approach to understanding long-term food security.

About the Research

Rice is an important staple food in Malaysia. However, the Malaysian rice industry is only able to satisfy about 75% of the local demand as the rest is compensated by imported rice. This ability to satisfy local demand with local production is called Self-sufficiency, and experts have noted that achieving an above average self-sufficiency level will be unlikely to be achieved in the years to come.

This gap between the local demand and local production demands us to look into the complexities of the Malaysian rice industry. Various interconnected factors, such as paddy cultivation area, productivity, population growth, consumption patterns, and imports, collectively influence the country's self-sufficiency level. Understanding how these factors interact over time is essential for developing effective policies to improve rice production and reduce dependence on imports.

Methodology

A System Dynamics simulation model (Stock Flow Diagram) was developed to represent the interactions between food production, consumption, population growth, and policy interventions. Data from relevant literature and publicly available sources were used to define model parameters and validate simulation behavior.

Results

Based on both interventions to increase self-sufficiency level, the better intervention is to increase person per hectare from 100,000 to 300,000. Increasing person per hectare will directly decrease the rate of land being converted to housing projects, which will increase the areas that can be used as granary areas for rice production.
